•     Desire for Control.
People who feel an internal sense of control tend to be mentally healthier and more successful (see: this Psychology Today article). So, as the University of Texas study notes, when customers feel that they are controlling their experience with a business, they are more satisfied with it. You may notice that the vast majority of personalization strategies don’t actually give the customer more control. However, the illusion of control comes from having an experience tailored to them, and it is powerful enough to give customers the feeling of control, and the psychological payoffs which come with that.

•     Cognitive Load.
Modern times have been dubbed the ‘Information Age’ – we are surrounded by more information than ever. However, this can be overwhelming. The term cognitive load refers to the amount of information we can process at once. Frequently, we are bombarded with information competing for our attention, and this places a heavy cognitive load on our attention. According to the University of Texas study, because personalization curates the most relevant information for a customer and brings it forward for each customer, it simplifies the cognitive processing they’re being asked to perform and supports a longer attention span.
